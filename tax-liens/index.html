Tax Liens and Bankruptcy

An IRS or state tax lien is a separate creature from the underlying tax debt. Discharging the tax debt does NOT release the lien. This page covers how tax liens are treated in bankruptcy and the strategies for addressing them.

The two-track problem

For a debtor with a tax lien:

  • Personal liability for the tax can be discharged in bankruptcy if the three-rule timing tests are satisfied
  • The lien itself typically survives bankruptcy and remains attached to the debtor's property

Result: the IRS can no longer collect from the debtor personally (post-discharge), but the lien continues to affect property the debtor owns at filing.

What the lien attaches to

An IRS Notice of Federal Tax Lien (NFTL) attaches to all of the taxpayer's property and rights to property at the time the lien arises. This includes:

  • Real estate (land, residence)
  • Personal property (vehicles, household goods)
  • Future property acquired before lien release
  • Wages, in some circumstances

Stripping the lien in Chapter 13

Under § 506(d), a lien can be voided to the extent it secures an amount in excess of the value of the property. In Chapter 13, this can be done through plan confirmation.

Where the tax lien attaches to a property and the lien value exceeds property equity, the under-secured portion can be stripped and treated as unsecured debt (subject to general dischargeability rules).

Discharging vs. lien-strip in practice

For a debtor with $50,000 IRS tax debt, $30,000 home equity, and a $50,000 tax lien:

  • The $20,000 over-secured portion remains as a lien
  • The $30,000 secured by actual home equity remains as a secured claim
  • If the debt itself is dischargeable (timing rules satisfied), personal liability is discharged but the $30,000 secured portion survives as a lien on the home
  • The debtor can sell the home and pay off the lien from proceeds, or wait until lien expiration (10 years from assessment, with extensions)

Lien expiration

The federal tax lien expires 10 years from the date of assessment (per IRC § 6502). Assessments can be re-extended in specific circumstances, but the basic 10-year clock runs absent action by the IRS.

State tax liens

State tax liens follow state-specific rules. In most states they parallel the IRS framework but can have different durations, recording requirements, and lien-strip availability. State-specific analysis is required.

Practical strategies

  • Discharge the tax debt's personal liability in bankruptcy (if dischargeable)
  • Use Chapter 13 plan to strip the under-secured portion of the lien
  • Wait out the 10-year lien expiration if practical (rare; usually too long)
  • Sell encumbered property and apply proceeds to the lien
  • Offer in compromise (OIC) outside bankruptcy if the IRS will accept reduced payment
  • For state liens, check state-specific lien-release procedures
